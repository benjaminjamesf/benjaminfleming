Autonomous Secure Housing

ASH is an interaction design project exploring how cities could interface with autonomous technologies in response to gendered violence.

It was created in collaboration with Bethany Koulyras & Nathan Judges for the University of Sydney's Interaction Design Studio where it was awarded Best Research Design.

The brief:

You are tasked with mapping the interactions that will be required to interface with autonomous vehicles in future cities.

Initial research into this space had us intrigued by the evolving phenomenon of bias in autonomous systems and their predicted negative economic impact on women.

We sought to explore how these systems could be better utilized for and by women through the lens of a prominent issue in Australia: gendered violence.

Research

To understand this problem space we first asked ourselves what we needed to know, who should we engage with, and how? From this we designed a triangulated research approach to uncover the current processes, technology, and problems in this sector across distinct stakeholder groups:

Police Victims Services
Semi-structured interviews, contextual inquiry Narrative inquiry, secondary analysis Semi-structured interviews, contextual inquiry

Analysis

After conducting our primary research we analyzed the data we'd collected using affinity diagramming and problem mapping to distill what we'd learnt into sets of user needs and design tools like personas and current state storyboards.

Our analysis efforts quickly became incredibly complex. This problem is incredibly multi-faceted, there are no ‘one size fits all’ solutions. Our research covered distinct intersections of the issue, all with differing needs of an interface and goals/processes that sometimes directly competed with one another. How could we support each stakeholders approach while achieving better outcomes?

Ideation

We broke up our stakeholders and paired them with vehicles using forced association techniques, generating nearly 100 concepts. Embodying our personas - we analyzed trends in our solutions and shaved them down in a knockout process until three remained.

To select the best concept, we first employed generative methods to flesh out their features and benefits and then semi-realized all three through storyboards and early paper prototypes.

User testing

Users were involved at every step of our process. We created task-based think aloud tests for each of our iterations, using sketching and the Pop by Marvel app to test our lower fidelity wireframes. Their feedback informed our decisions when developing higher fidelity mockups in Sketch and then interactive prototypes using Framer.

We also illustrated multiple scenarios and walked participants through these during testing and used these responses to further our concept. This allowed us to select Autonomous Secure Housing (ASH) as the chosen solution. It met the most user needs and supported the best outcomes for all stakeholders. We were also interested in designing dual interfaces that interact with the one service and exploring how they'd work in harmony.

Iteration

The interfaces were developed through further research of ride sharing and personal safety applications, and current semi-autonomous vehicle displays. As the intended audience would be broad - we aimed to maintain consistency with current UI patterns with an emphasis on providing legible & semantic interactions.

Problem space & concept solutions

Women do not leave dangerous situations for diverse reasons

Fear of repercussions from the police to either victim or partner, fear of the unknown or the safety of their children. Victims rarely want to leave a situation - they just want the violence to stop. It takes on average eight attempts to leave a violent relationship.

ASH would provide an avenue for leaving - or even just a safe space to think about doing so - without disclosing to anyone, not even the police.

Even if they did want to leave - there could be nowhere to go

Refuges, emergency centres and crisis accomodations are underfunded and at capacity. The wait for more permanent accomodation can be upwards of ten years.

Whilst ASH is not intended as a forever home - a fleet of them may be able to lessen the load on existing resources and free up space for others. Knowing there is a private safe space could encourage women to leave sooner and assess their options, preventing a critical decision to stay.

Police may not be the best first response to domestic violence

Police as the first responders to domestic violence bring with it inherent challenges. Some women have been cited as unhappy with the way their cases are handled, particularly in the evidence captured by police, timeliness of responses, and the attitudes of first responding officers.

Research suggests their approach could deepen distrust of services, reduce the likelihood of conviction and culturally reinforce an idea of “acceptable violence,” cyclically preventing instances of violence and creating cultures that aggravate the issue as whole.

ASH would provide a safe space for women separate from police. Women would be more likely to seek help if the response were less aggressive - and we hope to explore ways ASH could actually help this evidence collection process and assist in the conviction of offenders while protecting those who need it.

ASH is an autonomous secure housing bus intended as a first response unit for those needing to flee violence

Booking a bus follows familiar design language to other ride sharing apps - with most important information like bus availability immediately visible.

Safety is it's number one priority

When the bus nears - your phone functions as a key - the lock animation changing to alert you of access.

It can be disguised to preserve secrecy

To maintain anonymity at all times - the Hide My Screen button is persistent across all screens. Tapping on it changes the app into a simple yet functioning game of tic tac toe. Hard pressing the screen for three seconds switches back to home.

Once aboard you're always presented with reassurance of security

Users can view constant feedback about the vehicles status - its sensors displayed as a persistent animation on all screens with battery, speed and exterior conditions floating above. The bus status can be further explored through the charge tab - showing more information like km remaining.

You're in complete control of the ambient conditions

Users can then control all ambient aspects of the bus from this screen. - Clicking an icon once brings up contextual quick actions where users can change broad options like cold or warm - pressing more drills further into these controls where they can change each aspect singularly.

ASH allows you to choose the path, helping you along the way

Enter in custom addresses or access resources on what to do next - and how to get there.
