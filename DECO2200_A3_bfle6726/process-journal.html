Process Journal

Autonomous vehicle technologies are going to shift the future city, far sooner than you’d think. We sought to explore the way people are going to interface with these future technologies to solve present problems. The following is a journal exploring our entire design process from A to V.

Meet the team

At the heart of every good project is a good team. Prior to even viewing a project brief I knew that working with the right people would be the key to our success. Group assignments once made me feel like I was being penalised week one (bless the collective sigh when some tutor edicts that groups are random), but after working in some crash hot teams I’ve entered the light and now see how they’re a necessity. Our team had worked together before and we were into our dynamic – spending twilight hours together was fun even when it wasn’t. We knew we had many of those hours ahead, making for an easy decision to take on the Interaction Design studio together.

We named ourselves Bed N Breakfast – mostly because it was an immediate acronym, partially because we were full service: approaching a project from all angles and producing all the deliverables.

Tasked with delivering a team charter, we agreed upon some ground rules:

  • We will communicate openly about our ideas always - no holds barred and constructive critique only.
  • Forward plan all elements of our project and democratically decide how to approach them in regards to the methods, contexts, and time slots in the week we will meet up.
  • Don’t be shit!

Armed with our teams dynamism and shared excitement to dive in - we began our deep dive into all things interaction.

Oliver from Mobile Experience lead our class on rapid sketching and ideation, seeing us sketch out a variety of doors in exploration of how each is the same object which solves a different problem. He emphasised that iteration of ideas lead to novel designs and better products through a constant idea generation and refinement process. This lead to us scoping out the current state of things in autonomous vehicles, researching the current who’s who and what’s, and generating some early ideas of products we’d like to design interfaces for.

A brief unpacking

The design brief tasked us with the following:

Your task as interaction designers is to map out the interactions that will be required to assist people in interacting with autonomous vehicles in future cities.

After unpacking the brief as a group we mapped out all the possible future uses of autonomous vehicles that we could think of.

Mapping out opportunities

To narrow down our brainstorming session we first devised a set of overarching groups of services in a city environment like Accessibility, Transport and Safety. This was to give a bit of structure to our ideas and help us avoid the initial blank page effect. We went around the table and put down anything we could think of, spitballing of each other and linking related concepts. This gave us a valuable way to assess where each member wanted to take our project and culminated in three concepts we wanted to pursue:

  1. An autonomous high speed autobahn: a high speed vehicle corridor linking Eastern Australian cities and regional hubs, replacing our reliance on domestic flights. This could either be a specialised corridor where passengers used their own private vehicles that became autonomous upon entering the highway to achieve high speeds or a transit-stop type approach with stations in cities like a personalised train service.
  2. Autonomous fast food delivery robots: a modular fleet of delivery carts for big chain fast food companies with compartments and features that can be customised according to the businesses needs. We were interested in exploring how the interfaces of each element would interplay (specifically from a customer, store employee, and enterprise facing contexts) with each other and the carts functionalities at massive scale.
  3. Modular public transport systems: Uber Pool’s arrival in Australia highlighted that public transit systems in high density urban areas may not be serving their residents as well as they could. We wondered if creating smaller and faster methods of transit for city passengers headed in similar directions could work and how interfaces that needed to be used by any and all members of the public would need to be designed.

After researching different aspects of our proposed ideas we chose the autobahn idea as our chosen concept to create. We felt it had the most impact and potential in changing the way future cities would transport people. Governments had already considered constructing a high speed rail line between Melbourne, Sydney and Brisbane, and the Sydney > Melbourne flight path is the second busiest route in the world. Airplane travel is notoriously unfriendly to the environment and its use is growing much faster than our ability to innovate on how it is fuelled.

Urbanisation and lack of housing are pertinent problems in Australian cities: we all flock to eastern capitals, negatively impacting the environment, causing major housing stress and gentrification thus deepening class divides. We move to these cities for a variety of benefits - what if they were easier to access and did not require you to live in a 2000 - 2400 postcode? A green corridor of autonomous vehicles could allow Australians to own a home (currently a pipe dream for 99% of us) in a regional centre and still commute to work or school in the CBD without the negative impacts.

Re-problematized

In order for us to realise this concept to interface for, we first had to understand who we were designing for. Under Oliver’s direction we used the internet and our knowledge to populate a stakeholder map of all the possible actors in our concepts domain. This was to help us formulate a research plan for the first stage of our project by allowing us to select participants we could easily access to study.

Mapping out opportunities

We also began talking our concept through with our peers to gather feedback on it’s validity and scope how others generally thought about it. Our lecturer Hamish loved the concept and how we had come to a considered choice so soon - he could clearly see its potential. However Sophie (our tutor(hi lol!)) felt quite the opposite. She too loved that we were focused on getting a solution but felt we were jumping right to the end of the process way way too early - if we had a solution already then we were headed in the wrong direction.

We both did not want to pigeonhole ourselves and achieve non-novel designs from the outset. Sophie emphasised that as designers we shouldn’t be diving straight into an idea that anyone on the street could just conjure up, we needed to find and understand a problem before we spurt solutions. We immediately knew her advice to be correct. So we scrapped our solution and opened the floor to a space we’d be passionate exploring (properly this time).

Mapping out opportunities

We approached this in much the same way as our first concept map: what problems do we face in Sydney that we assume others face to? We quickly realised that as privileged inner city kids - we may not be the best people to ask. Rather than draw problems outta our minds by sheer force, we looked at Government policies. As the parties are elected by Australian people, we felt they’d at least know a little bit about broad problems they face (even if they didn’t intend on actually doing anything about them). We devised three “problem themes” from our earlier solution and then drew out the areas from there.

Exploring who we could access for each domain

While waste management was a strong contender, we chose to embrace complexity with our chosen space: domestic violence. It was a problem that intrigued all of us as it was women focused and a vocal issue amongst a lot of our friends. I also had a friend who was a case manager in a domestic violence shelter and I knew she’d be really into a project like ours.

Research plan

Now that we had chosen an area to explore, we needed to formulate a plan for our research. After replacing the usual cursory meme scroll with topic reading I came across the United Nations “How to design projects to end violence against women and girls.” We utilised this toolkit to populate a new stakeholder map:

Stakeholder map of domestic violence actors

We did this to see which stakeholders we could access to perform research activities. Domestic violence (DV) is a broad, multi-faceted issue, there are many different services and people collaborating together. Hendrik Mueller, a user researcher from Google lead our tutorial on research methods, explaining that a triangulated approach of techniques that were valid and reliable yielded the best results.

We selected participants in the same approach: I would be focusing on social workers and crisis support services. Nathan would be looking at the police and Bethany at victim and homelessness support centres.

Our groups research objective: To assess and understand the factors involved with domestic violence in Sydney. To achieve this we individually devised a set of research questions we sought to answer. As I was targeting social services, I wanted to know the status quo of the sector and gain an insider understanding:

  • What services are available?
  • Whats the status quo in the social services sector for this issue?
  • What are the processes for assisting a victim?
  • What technologies are being utilised?
  • What barriers are present to victims and services?

To answer these questions I selected three research techniques: contextual inquiry, observation and semi structured interviews coupled with intense secondary research on the topic with an Australian focus.

I felt that observational techniques of these services and witnessing the technology and processes they used combined with narrative recounts from social workers would form a robust base of data to create design artefacts like personas and storyboards from. Tools such as those would be crucial for our concept development further down the line.

An interview

My first research activity was to perform secondary analysis - I wanted to know a little about the processes and terminology in the space, as well as gain an introduction to the topic so I was prepared to conduct my first interview. I summarised the most interesting findings for my visual report:

  • 1 in 6 Australian women have experienced partner violence (ABS, 2016), Indigenous women are 35x more at risk (Aus. Gov, 2010)
  • Causation is complex, majority of studies and related bodies empirically agree that gender inequality is the key determinant (APH, 2015)
  • Elements of abuse extend far beyond the physical and current technologies are increasingly facilitating them (Woodlock, 2016)
  • Fragmentation and gaps between social and government services, law systems and enforcement, create barriers for assistance and prevention (ALRC, NSWLRC, 2010)
  • Domestic violence and homelessness are intrinsically linked; fear of homelessness and housing insecurity prevent women from leaving dangerous situations (Cameron, 2015)
  • The most prevalent cited cause for womens homelessness is domestic violence (McLaren, 2013)
  • More than 115,000 people are homeless in Australia, 44% are women. Homelessness increased 70% in NSW in the most recent census (ABS, 2016).

I had also contacted my friend Jess: A case manager and social worker from the Women’s and Girls’ Emergency Centre (WAGEC), a specialist homelessness service providing crisis accomodation and both in house and outreach support for women and families escaping domestic violence. We arranged to meet up for pho and have a chat about her work. I created a set of base questions I had hoped to answer with our interview:

  • What are the current processes when an at risk woman approaches your service?
  • What role does your job and your organisation have in this context?
  • What technologies are currently utilized, and how do you feel about them?
  • What are the barriers you face when assisting clients, and what barriers do at risk women face when interacting with your service?

The interview went really well - the pho even better. My jaw was at the floor for majority of the hour long interview, Jess was really passionate about her work and the social sector and the current state of things was… grim, to put it lightly.

Government policy makers had made jobs like hers nigh impossible over the past decade, creating violent feedback loops that simultaneously created, exacerbated and refused to solve the domestic violence phenomena. She also reinforced my findings on the link between DV and homelessness: a lot of her clients current goals were finding housing, the waitlist for public housing is more than ten years (even in an emergency). She had given me an invaluable firsthand account of the scenario at hand and a lot to think about.

Jess put me in touch with the Newtown Neighbourhood Centre, an outreach support service for those at risk of homelessness in Sydney. I got in contact with them and began planning for a contextual inquiry at their office.

Contextual inquiry

I had arranged to perform contextual inquiry and interviews with the Newtown Neighbourhood Centre, a generalist homeless service providing predominantly outreach support for those at risk of homelessness in the Inner West of Sydney.

I’d been emailing with a few case managers there and before I’d even arrived there was some.. issues… with our study. Jess must’ve told them the end goal of our research.

Her qualms and reasoning were still interesting and valid - I made sure she knew this. Her main response to the project - that autonomous technologies would have an affect on her ability to be empathetic, intrigued me, and I hoped to understand this more when I visited the office. I had formulated a plan for the study with a set of questions I hoped to answer to give a slight structure to my observation. I wanted to observe social workers in their work environment for an hour; asking questions and positing scenarios to them whilst taking notes and photographs of what is done and said. Questions to be answered and focal points to observe:

  • What are the processes involved in your day to day servicing of clients?
  • What technologies are currently utilised in your service? And how do you feel about them?
  • What outreach services do you provide and how do you provide them?
  • Roleplaying a new client intake, observing required tech, forms, and systems

This was to observe direct responders to this issue in the context of the workspace they provide support from, to get a firsthand understanding of the interfaces and technologies these users interact with everyday to better inform our future designs. The case managers who worked at the centre were incredibly forthcoming and interested in the project, the work they did was integral to the fabric of the city and they seemed like a lil family - the office had a warming vibe.

Aretha Franklin had passed away that morning so her music punctuated the visit and a picture of her had been pasted on the wall. They managed to maintain this warmth even when the work they were doing (essentially assisting those in our community most at risk) rarely had positive outcomes, I had mad respect for them and their passion for good.

The need to maintain empathy was echoed in their use of paper materials. The case manager explained that she couldn’t simply be tapping on an iPad when she meeting with someone - paper was important both to clients and to workers who had built the industry with paper in hand.

The centre is heavily reliant on paper for most forms (both internal documents like intake forms and intra-agency communications like housing applications).
Client information is also primarily paper based and is stored manually in filing cabinets and folders.
Digitisation of these documents is case worker dependant - finding a required file involves getting up from the workspace and hoping it’s where you expected to be - adding time to an already time constrained role.

The digital systems they had were government lead and lacked proper implementation - the new CIMS (Client information management) system was down when we arrived.

This reliance on paper both made sense and seemed bizarre to me - it wasn’t that technology would necessarily negate the use of paper, the tech they’d been provided with didn’t mesh with the work they were doing.

The centre's coverage area - other services and POI's were marked with stickers.

Next I had to make sense of the breadth of information I had learned and translate it into actionable needs for our technology development. To distill everything I employed affinity diagramming - pulling out affinities from all of the data that I could then group into themes and create hierarchies (the user needs). I then collated all of my findings into a visual report to present to my peers and to corroborate with my trusted research pals to start generating some concepts.

Cheap post-its = floor diagramming.

The primary needs I summarised for my report:

  • Cultural attitudes towards these issues are both causal factors and a hinderance to intervention
  • Time and resistance to change are constraints on social workers ability to help
  • Government policies make it harder for both social workers and the victims they work with
  • Finding suitable accomodation is incredibly difficult
  • The lack of housing exacerbates domestic violence.

“The strain that exists on specialist services and their capacity to help women would cease if there were simply a place people could go.”

Generating solutions

After presenting our visual reports and regrouping as a team - our first step was to combine our research and come up with a best plan of attack to create an interface in response to our uncovered problems. Stephanie Grace, an interface designer, lead our tutorial on rapid prototyping and wireframing, focusing on making sure you’re on message and constantly asking for feedback from peers. Her advice lead our next project activity - making sense of our data and generating concepts.

We attempted to map out the roles and processes involved in responding to domestic violence. This was not an easy feat - we had so much research to sift through and the different stakeholders were in competition.

As we each covered different aspects (like the police and social workers) we were kind’ve role playing the areas we covered, arguing and explaining our actors issues as them. When we noticed this we took it a step further and added our personas into the mix and forcibly roleplayed as them which allowed us to deepen this explanation and be more empathetic towards each other. Our goal with this activity was to get everyone on the one page and to generate concepts we’d like to pursue. We were definitely on the one whiteboard but we quickly became frustrated with our early ideas.

They were either too narrow and garnered isolated benefits or far too broad and could be applicable to the general population.

Some of them even bordered on sexist - we were generating concepts but they just weren’t hitting the mark for us. We didn’t want to have done all of this in depth research to end up with a pedestrian solution. To overcome this creative stagnation, we decided upon the forced association design method to generate a swathe of concepts (no matter how ridiculous).

Forced association is technique were a design problem is broken down into variables and components which are then “forced” together to generate new ideas.

We created a set of stakeholder cards (Social workers, Victims, Courts etc) together and individually created up to thirty vehicle cards.

I looked at lists of vehicles as I’m an educated adult who couldn’t think of thirty without help. But I did learn that a Zamboni is apparently an ice rink cleaner; it unfortunately didn’t make the cut.

Stephanie Grace introduced us to the problem statement method for interface design: How could X use Y to achieve Z. We co-opted these statements for the activity and forced the vehicle card with a stakeholder and created an autonomous concept for each one - generating and sketching twenty concepts. This alone didn’t help our project - the true a-ha moment came from meeting up again and analysing what we had come up with. After discussing all of our work - we analysed what we’d created and looked at trends in our thinking, novel ideas and laughed at silly ones.

Bus takes victims to different workplaces, like a field trip, where they can learn about different careers, then takes children to school

- Beth was bullied for this weirdo concept.

Social workers could use semi-autonomous segways to quickly navigate inner-city environments

- Imagine that.

Shameless DTMBR plug

We picked out the three strong ideas each from this and then wrote them in a matrix. Again we embodied our personas and carved down the ideas to three. We had hoped at this stage to have just two concepts to explore but couldn’t decide on which to choose.

The activity had made us 360 from regret at selecting such a complex problem to again revelling in it.

We needed an extra set of eyes on the concepts to help - causing us to decide to move forward with all three ideas and let our users decide which were best.

Using a persona lead decision matrix to knock out ideas as a group

In order to do this our concepts had to go from single sentences to realised ideas. We needed to illustrate them as storyboards and have an understanding about what they would and wouldn’t do. To achieve this we employed another technique: brain writing 6-3-5. This activity was the most helpful in our entire process - each idea was written at the top of a sheet of demarcated paper.

A timer was set and each member wrote down a feature or use case for each concept. On the buzz we rotated the sheets and reflected and built upon each others thoughts. The time constraint meant we didn’t have time to get caught up in what would or wouldn’t work, you just had to get it all out as soon as it came to you.

This had fleshed out the statements into fully fledged solutions - our next step was to create storyboards and early interface ideas to get feedback from our users and peers. We’d come from hating ourselves for choosing such a tricky space to again being confident in our project so we were super excited to realise our designs.

Illustrating Concepts

Now that we had some concepts with a little weight behind them - our next step was to illustrate them, think about how their interfaces would function, and then gather feedback from users and peers about their utility.

We each took ownership of a concept and tasked eachother with creating a storyboard explaining the idea. The focus was showing their functionality with an emphasis on explaining context - we were aware that most of our peers would be in the dark about domestic violence processes (as we were prior to our research undertaking).

Case managers are still heavily reliant on paper forms & keeping track of clients manually, their digital tools typically don’t make their jobs easier. The office assistant is a roving scanner/printer imbued with optical character recognition to automatically digitise paper documents. This is paired with process based client management software that interfaces with government agencies securely and instantly to achieve faster, positive outcomes for managers and victims.
Victims of domestic violence in refuges are often financially isolated and from poorer educational backgrounds. The maintenance cart is an educational toolbox summoned to a clients location that could instruct users trade knowledge (like fixing the door on a cabinet) or in creation of artworks like pottery or jewellery intertwined with mindfulness techniques. This would both teach them new skills and provide a sense of calm and purpose in an easy to understand, hands-on approach.
Women at risk of domestic violence don’t leave dangerous situations due to fear of police repercussion, lack of resources, and simply that there may not be a safe place to go. The secure housing bus is an anonymous motor home that can be called via app to provide temporary crisis accomodation, resource education and transport to necessary services for victims.

The social worker assistant bot was my concept to develop and test. It was also my personal hero concept - having witnessed social worker officers at their place of work had really cemented their need for a technological solution that made sense to them. Their existing tools were not needs focused and were developed by the government for all social enterprises. This meant they were painting all services and their employees with the same brush, broadly solving some problems, but lacking any ability to modularize use for differing service types or encompass the diversity of actors utilizing it. They desperately need tools that intersect with their current workflows without a burden - anything new in this low-tech space cannot add another layer of work to an already underfunded & understaffed service.

I sketched out possible interface concepts for the social worker bot based on my own experiences with process-based enterprise CRM software. I knew from my research that mobile app use is on the upward trend with social workers and that high fidelity apps like Uber and Facebook proliferated the screens of their personal lives - they were used to premium experiences and had high expectations of mobile applications. I wanted to offer a hi-def experience and change their perception of what an enterprise software had to look like.

These initial sketches were to explore the information architecture of the mobile app used to interact with the bot and see which features made sense.

Showing these sketches to my peers yielded some valuable feedback, primarily that the slide between panes view I had started with would probably be confusing and make it difficult to know which page you were on and how to get to the next. The breakdown "trees" of tasks and flyover menu to access the bot was well received - I worked on refining it for a set of paper prototypes that I could get into the hands of users to test.

As a group we decided upon think-aloud testing as our primary method of gathering user feedback. We needed a way to evaluate our early interfaces hard and fast with as many participants as possible. Think aloud testing is quickly understood by participants, requires minimum resources and can be conducted rapidly with robust, actionable results when planned correctly.

We first designed two abstract tasks (higher goal to accomplish) with two sub-concrete tasks (hard steps to goal completion) based on our use-cases for the interface. We then digitised our paper prototypes using the Pop by Marvel application and tested these amongst ourselves to ensure they made sense. Next we selected participants for the testing based on the different functionalities of each application, and then conducted the testing with these groups.

I selected friends of mine who were familiar with enterprise CRM software for the testing - I thought they'd be best as they would be bringing a set of learned expectations to the table. The main qualms with the interface were the use of the Marvel application - two subjects "couldn't wrap their heads around it" and kept trying to swipe through the app as if they were in the camera roll (like - hello!? Tap on the buttons like you do in every-other-app-on-ya-damn-phone!) and the other hated the display. Using X boxes for placeholders confused her a lot, and at one point she paused and literally said "I could do this if you didn't draw them so shit." Love you too babe.

Apart from the general rudeness around my sketching ability - the test fared well-ish. Once teething problems were sorted - the information architecture and use of the bots flyover menu were well understood.

This user had been a victim of my user testing many-a-times - here she is showing us how she learned to cheat.

After conducting my first round of user testing, my next step was analyzing the results and discussing them with my trusted research pals in order to come to a decision about which concept to pursue further.

A hero concept emerges

Our trio collated all of our testing data and discussed the feedback we’d gathered from our users. We STILL were having difficulty choosing a hero solution - all of our concepts tested well, people liked them, and we each loved our individual ideas as if they were our digital children and wouldn’t let them go.

On our morning smoko the week our wireframes were due, we bit the bullet and did what children would do:

“On the count of three, everyone just say which idea they think is the best"

(yes, if you must ask, it had come to this).

1, 2, 3:

Secure Housing Bus!

In unison! Finally. Why this choice? We agreed the software side of the social worker assistant was sick - it was just hampered by the fact that its vehicular element was essentially a printer on wheels. Our peers understood the software but baulked at how ridiculous it looked. Before it’s time.

The maintenance cart as a product was amazing - it just didn’t fit well in our product space and came off as a more premium service of the future. I also struggled with its lightly sexist undertones when applied in our problem space. Again - it’s vehicle-ness was a product that didn’t need wheels, on wheels.

Initially we were wary of the secure housing bus - it was one of the first concepts we thought of, and it was seemingly a pedestrian approach to the problem. However - conceptually it made perfect sense to users. We enjoyed designing multiple interfaces for different contexts that worked in harmony.

It also performed the strongest in the user evaluation, and after performing deeper secondary research we realised we could take our first thought concept and turn it into a laser focused, multi-faceted solution.

An illustration of the concept by the illustrious cutie Nathan Judges

After some deeper secondary research into the topic we discovered that the bus goes deeper than we thought. I got really into reading affected women's narratives and the analysis of them - in doing so I learnt that police as the response to domestic violence may not be the answer.

Police as the first responders to domestic violence brings with it inherent challenges. Women more often than not are unhappy with the way their cases are handled, particularly in the evidence captured by police, timeliness of responses, and the attitudes of first responding officers. Research suggest their approach could deepen distrust of services, reduce the likelihood of conviction and culturally reinforce an idea of “acceptable violence,” cyclically preventing instances of violence and creating cultures that aggravate the issue as whole.

ASH would provide a safe space for women unobstructed by police. Women would be more likely to seek help if this response was less aggressive - and we hope to explore ways ASH could actually help this evidence collection process and assist in the conviction of offenders while protecting those who need it.

We set out to conduct yet another round of user testing before we began bringing the interface of our concept to pixelated life. I created another set of paper prototypes (neatly this time) and tested them with users to help inform our research troupes high fidelity mockups.

Bringing ASH to life

After another round of testing on our paper prototypes, we were ready to realize our concepts interface. We ditched the secure housing bus name and gave her a new moniker: ASH (short for Autonomous Secure Housing). Performing several rounds of testing had given us a wealth of information to go over, yet users were completing our concrete tasks fairly easily. We had iterated a fair amount of times and we were gripping what was and wasn't appropriate for this interface. We also loved creating two interfaces that interact with the one service: one for booking on a mobile application and another for the bus' on board display.

Working out how to keep the language and use of these interfaces unified was difficult when we worked apart, but when we brought the two together and worked together they kind've just fell into place.

We studied a plethora of current technologies to inform our design - primarily Tesla's iterations on their in car display system, Mercedes Benz' birds-eye view functions on their premium cars, and Google's self driving car's sensor animations. Beth and I accidentally watched some tech enthusiast nerd review Tesla's in car display for a full 28 minutes. Did we learn something? .. Yes. Do we recommend it? If "post-middle age virgin man harps on about innocuous window wiper feature for seven minutes" sounds like your ASMR - jump on in.

We used a combination of Sketch, Photoshop and XD to come to our first set of mockups:

We then conducted our largest round of user testing with 8 users - before we transformed our wireframes into an interactive prototype, we wanted to know what we would create worked perfectly.

Our testing proved we were onto something - all participants loved our app and they completed all the tasks with fitnesse. We had minor hiccups to correct (like the ellipses icon for more settings) but mostly - we were green lit for the finale.
