DesignOne

DesignOne is a business innovation project aimed at leveraging universities existing resources to benefit their communities through design education outreach programs.

It was created Autumn 2018 in collaboration with Soomin Lee for the University of Sydney's Innovation Design Studio where it will be furthered as an Honours research project in 2020.

The brief:

Identify a design problem and develop a business case for the launch of a new venture to pitch to a panel of business leaders as a concept that could be realised as a commercial solution.

An independent review of Australian schooling systems highlighted our declining academic performance.

To combat this decline the report had two key priorities:

Equip every student to be a creative, connected and engaged learner in a rapidly changing world

Cultivate an innovative and continuously improving education system which emphasises collaboration between schools and their communities


Research projects aimed at equipping our students with these 21st century skills have been hugely successful - but they were not continued commercially due to resource constraints or systemic support.

We have kids who are in dire need of an up skill, already strained teachers and schools with scarce resources. You have tertiary students like us who know of this problem first hand and want to make a difference - but how?

Finding a domain

At the beginning of this project our team first researched and generated multiple problem spaces that we could design within. A recurring theme in our thinking was innovation education (or the lack we had individually experienced). After extensive secondary research we found that this was an emerging issue not just for ourselves but for Australia’s education system. We used generative methods to scope out dozens of solutions in a short time frame.

One solution, inspired by recent developments in machine learning supported interactions, was chosen to develop.

First concept

We fleshed out the products business case with multiple rounds through a product development matrix and contrasted existing services with a SWOT analysis.

Designerly was a tablet application augmented with machine learning that would allow for real-time interactive lessons on design concepts. We created a small pitch and illustrated the concept through storyboards to gather feedback from stakeholders.

Stakeholder feedback

The school teachers and design educators we pitched to reinvigorated our problem - design theory and innovative thinking are missing from the school system and are necessary for the new business landscape, but they found the proposed solution to be so-so.

They were concerned that it targeted upskillers and self educators (our early prototypes and analysis methods lead us to believe that targeting the school system would limit our scalability and that the school sector had too many legislative roadblocks to overcome). The issue was that our current iteration was focused on the wrong facets of the problem - it was tech heavy and lacked an immersive human aspect that this type of education required.

This was an eight week design sprint and our project was running dangerously low on time - we were very close to canning the concept and pursuing a simpler domain.

In a last attempt to save ourselves starting over, we mapped everything we’d learnt from our interviews and research and then used this to run through multiple iterations of the business model canvas - shifting the core focus of our business in each variation.

Business model canvas

The partner lead variant of our business model canvas allowed us to reframe the problem and pivot from a technology product to a human lead service. We had struggled to devise a way we could employ design educators in a way that schools could afford at scale. Similar programs that had been implemented in the past had been successful in that they were enjoyed by teachers and students, but faced major roadblocks in securing funding and ongoing systemic support.

Instead of creating an entirely new business that was profit driven - what if we could utilise current students at universities through the creation of a design education subject as a community outreach program?

It would provide educational benefits to the tertiary students and fill the gaps that schools were missing in a cost effective way. The university would be incentivised through positive branding and the opportunity to increase student interest in their institution - we could also develop the subject in a way that enables data collection to drive the universities education research goals.

To validate our refreshed idea - we again created a mini-pitch and illustrated the concept. Rather than storyboard, we drafted example lesson plans for the school students across different age groups and a subject outline for those at university. We interviewed current design students and found a definite thirst for a community subject of this type but there were concerns about how they would deliver the content - causing us to create a tangible toolkit with the lesson plans and materials required on simple cards and mini-booklets.

Testing the concept

We then presented our new concept to design educators, education researchers and the universities community outreach centre. It was incredibly well received and they all wanted to know how they could help us get the service beyond an assignment and on the market. They emphasised that for us to secure funding the pitch needed to focus on holistically communicating the experience and this communication needed to impress.

To achieve this we created a prototype of the services experience - first by creating the programs brand and voice and then using this to produce rendered prototypes of the toolkit and its contents, lanyards, pamphlets, and designOne student educator t-shirts. We distilled our findings and concept into a succinct pop animation and pitched our project to investors as if we were delivering one of the programs classes.

DesignOne was the most funded project of the mock startup battlefield and was invited to be continued as an honours project for the university in 2020.

Concept

DesignOne is an outreach program of the University of Sydney that utilises its design students to instil innovative creative education in schools in our community. It is an elective senior subject that supplies students with an educational framework and toolkit with age-tailored creative problem solving activities.

Groups of students are then paired with K-10 schools in their area to conduct design-thinking workshops with a class and their teacher. Grade three students could radically collaborate with the classic spaghetti marshmallow challenge - prototyping and testing their spindly houses and critically reflecting about the challenges they faced.

In grade six they’re interviewing and empathising with each other and creating a poster about a classmates story.

In grade ten they’re faced with school-based problem like “waiting for the canteen takes too long - how can we make it more efficient” - observing and understanding their peers and the space around them and then competing on a solution.

There are more than 800 government schools in the Sydney region and many of them lack a fit design skills program.

We surveyed the universities design students and found that most have already expressed their interest in a course of this type, and as the new design major grows we expect that many of those multi disciplinary students will also enrol, and could allow for volunteers from other faculties to bolster our mentor numbers.

We hope to entice schools into this program through the universities community outreach channels and through events like design-a-thon challenges were schools could compete on solving a problem in their community - showing the value of this education and that students will actually love it.

DesignOne would educate and excite school students and their teachers without straining their resources and encourage them to engage with and take pride in their community.

It prepares them and our university students for the future by emboldening their design leadership.

It creates positive branding for the university and bolsters tertiary interest in our school.

It generates actual data for our researchers and the education community at large.

It’s a community and a platform for our students, by our students, aimed at empowering all involved in Australia’s future.
