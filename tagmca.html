Tag MCA

Tag MCA is a user experience design project exploring how art galleries can reaffirm their relationship with visitors by connecting them with the collection and each other.

It was created Autumn 2018 in collaboration with Bethany Koulyras & Nathan Judges for the University of Sydney's User Experience Design Studio.

The brief:

Design an experience for a museum that builds upon the collection that the institution offers. It should improve the visitor experience, fulfil educational goals of the institution, and help the museum engage with new audiences and affirm their relationship with existing ones.

Tag MCA’s aim is to shift the perception of the MCA as a solely aesthetic and passive experience to one that visitors view as a leisurely, socially connected environment. We sought to augment this aesthetic experience with interactivity and play so that the museum retains its inspirational and educational qualities in a way that visitors will want to return to with another.

We wanted to break down preconceived barriers that guests create; the museum can be a stifling environment that does not enable social leisure and that you need to “know” art to have a good time. We utilised what the MCA already possesses: a breadth of curated modern art content, expansive and self-exploratory indoor exhibition rooms and the striking exterior architecture.

This was interweaved with our insights from user research into what visitors require from a place to warrant them to return to conceptualise a digital, tangible experience that emboldens their sociality and makes them want to visit time and time again.

+ Show design process

Context research

The Museum of Contemporary Art (MCA) was our space of choice - we liked the diversity of its collection and the spatial opportunities its sheer size would give us.

To get a feel of how visitors used this space we spent a day shadowing some and taking notes of how they interacted with it and asked others about their experience.

We then used this data to create a direct experience storyboard illustrating the current pain points and flow to create a frame of user needs.

Portion of direct experience storyboard.

Our early interviews about the gallery gave our research scope: most who visit the gallery only do so once and then only return in a blue moon. To empathise with the personal and social contexts of these single-visit users we designed a generative research project aimed at mapping out why people went to the art gallery, or rather - why go on an outing at all?

User research

To cast a wide net over the galleries audience, eight participants were recruited from varying backgrounds (local students, international visitors, parents and artists) who had visited the MCA just once in the past two years.

To prepare them for the co-design sessions they were each supplied with a sensitising booklet: a series of playful and provocative activities for acquiring textured personal reflection that scratches beyond the surface.

These were brought to two generative sessions where the participants completed make and say exercises: a context mapping technique using stimulating hands on activities with group discussion in order to uncover their emotional drivers and latent needs.

The first was a collage mapping exercise with a supplied axes and varying images and words. I moderated the sessions and ensured everyone contributed equally and furthered the discussion with probing questions - looking for patterns or interesting portions of their creations. The participants were asked:

What does going on an outing with people mean to you? What did it mean to you in the past when you were younger? And what does it mean to you now in the present?

The second was a timeline activity where the participants where asked to recall a recent outing with friends.

What are the steps involved in organising a day out with your group? How does it come about? How do you eventually all meet up? And how does the day unfold? Which parts do you like and dislike?

Analysis

Our generative sessions has generated swathes of data, crossing multiple topics about outings and art galleries. To analyse and make sense of a large amount of qualitative data we opted for statement card affinity diagramming: a bottom up method where data is first filtered for similar statements and grouped into a statement card to be clustered by patterns or trends.

Once we had extracted the visitors needs and recurring themes from our data, I synthesised the findings into a research visualisation. Following the 3-30-300 rule, I created an infographic that captured our research insights in an easily digestable and aesthetic format to be referred to when evaluating design decisions or as a stakeholder communication tool.

Concept generation

Based on our user & contexual research we decided upon the most important three broad themes to ideate around:

- Casual, laid back settings with ancillary activities are key to a good outing

- The connectedness that comes from shared reflection is the best part of the gallery

- I don’t know enough about art to fully immerse myself in a gallery

We sketched out multiple ideas and created a set of criteria using our data to assess them with using a decision matrix. We each chose two concepts and realized them through experience storyboards to be pitched to stakeholders and industry.

Portions of each concept had merit and others were lost on the stakeholders - the overwhelming response was to amalgamate features from both.

Prototyping

As the goal of the project was to create a digital experience prototype that stakeholders would invest in - we felt the most succint and impactful way to sell it was as a video prototype. We researched the technology required and created a specification for the concept, created a low-fidelity physical prototype & filmed the experience on location at the MCA. We used a blend of After Effects and projectors to emulate the experience in a quick and communicative way.

The prototype was presented to some of the members of the generative study and their feedback was used to create a user journey map contrasting the current state and post experience state at the gallery.

Tag MCA is a high impact digital experience for the art gallery.

It combines complex technologies into an intuitive tangible activity. Visitors equip themselves with a digital spray can that feels just like the real thing.

A unique yet unifying activity that leaves no trace.

The gallery is fitted with multiple digital spray walls. The can vibrates to alert when you approach one and the embedded LCD relays a simple task - accepting it creates a stencil on the wall. You press the nozzle with a familiar pssshhh and you're up there with someone who was paid to be.

As you move through the gallery, the activities are contextually based on the works around you - granting a different connection with the artists and allowing you and your friends to express together whilst keeping with the MCA's mission of educating the masses on all things contemporary.

Utilizing elements of flow and gamification psychology - users can both affect their environments whilst taking in new information and interacting with their peers.

As you exit the gallery your activities are conjoined into a new personal art piece to share online and entice a new friend to come along next time.
